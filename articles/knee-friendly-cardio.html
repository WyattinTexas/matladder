5 Knee-Friendly Cardio Alternatives That Actually Work

Your knees don't have to end your cardio life. These five options deliver real cardiovascular benefits without the pounding.

By The Mat Ladder Team 7 min read June 2026

For decades, running was the default cardio recommendation. Lace up your shoes, hit the pavement, get your heart rate up. Simple, free, effective.

Until your knees had something to say about it.

If you're reading this, there's a good chance you already know the feeling: that dull ache behind the kneecap after a jog. The stiffness the next morning. The slow realization that what used to be your go-to workout is now something your body is pushing back against.

You're not alone. By age 45, nearly half of all adults report some degree of knee discomfort during high-impact exercise. By 55, that number climbs higher. And while knee replacement surgery gets the headlines, the far more common story is subtler: millions of people simply stop doing cardio because the options they know about hurt.

That's a problem. Cardiovascular exercise isn't optional for long-term health. It reduces heart disease risk, manages blood pressure, maintains healthy weight, and — increasingly recognized — protects cognitive function as you age.

So here are five alternatives that deliver real cardio results while keeping your knees in the conversation.

2

Swimming

Excellent — Zero Impact

The classic recommendation, and for good reason. Water eliminates impact entirely. Your knees experience zero ground-reaction force, while your cardiovascular system gets a thorough workout. Lap swimming can burn 400-700 calories per hour depending on intensity and stroke.

Swimming also provides gentle resistance training for your entire body, which makes it one of the most complete exercise forms available. For people with significant knee damage or post-surgical limitations, it's often the first exercise a physical therapist will recommend.

StrengthsZero joint impact. Full-body workout. Well-researched. Great for rehab.
LimitationsRequires pool access. Ongoing membership costs. Doesn't train balance or agility. Technique matters a lot.
3

Cycling (Outdoor or Stationary)

Great — Low Impact

Cycling is low-impact by design. Your body weight is supported by the seat, so your knees handle minimal load. The circular pedaling motion is smooth and predictable, which most arthritic joints tolerate well. Both outdoor cycling and stationary bikes deliver solid cardiovascular training.

The caveat is bike fit. An improperly set-up bike — especially seat height and cleat position — can actually worsen knee problems. If you go this route, invest in a professional fitting, especially if you have existing knee issues.

StrengthsLow joint load. Scalable intensity. Can be social (group rides). Great for sustained cardio.
LimitationsSeat discomfort. Bike fit critical for knee safety. Doesn't train balance, agility, or upper body. Equipment cost or gym membership.
4

Elliptical Trainer

Good — Reliable Option

The elliptical mimics a running motion without the impact. Your feet never leave the pedals, so there's no ground strike to transmit force through your knees. Most models include arm handles for an upper-body component, and resistance is easily adjusted.

The downside is monotony. Ellipticals provide a fixed, linear movement pattern. There's no lateral motion, no quick direction changes, no cognitive challenge. You get your heart rate up, but you don't train the dynamic movement skills that matter for real-world function.

StrengthsNo impact. Easy to use. Adjustable resistance. Available in most gyms.
LimitationsFixed movement pattern. No agility or balance benefit. Requires gym or expensive home machine. Can feel tedious.
5

Rowing Machine

Good — Underrated

Rowing is a full-body, low-impact cardio option that's often overlooked. The sliding seat means your knees bend and extend with each stroke, but without impact — the motion is smooth and controlled. Rowing works your back, arms, core, and legs simultaneously, and it can produce a serious cardiovascular challenge in a short time.

Proper technique is important. Bad rowing form can stress your lower back, and the deep knee bend at the catch position may be uncomfortable for people with significant knee flexion limitations. Start with shorter sessions and focus on form.

StrengthsFull-body engagement. Low impact. Efficient calorie burn. Great for time-pressed workouts.
LimitationsDeep knee flexion required. Lower back risk with poor form. Equipment needed. No balance or agility training.

The Takeaway

There's no single perfect cardio replacement for running. Each option on this list has genuine strengths. But if you're choosing just one, consider what you're actually trying to accomplish. If it's pure cardiovascular conditioning, swimming and cycling are proven. If it's time efficiency, rowing is hard to beat.

But if you want cardio and the agility, balance, and coordination training that your body increasingly needs as you age, cushioned ladder drills occupy a unique space. They're the only option on this list that simultaneously trains your heart, your reflexes, and your feet — on a surface that doesn't punish your joints for showing up.

A note on mixing: The best long-term approach is combining two or three of these options. Swim twice a week, do ladder drills twice a week, and cycle on weekends. Variety keeps you engaged and trains different systems. Your knees will thank you for all of it.
